Aspen die-off alarms foresters

January 15, 2008
BY JOE HANEL | Journal Denver Bureau

DENVER - Aspen and pine trees are dying at alarming rates across the state, foresters said Monday.

The U.S. Forest Service revealed the results of its yearly aerial survey of dead trees. They found 500,000 acres of newly dead pine trees, killed off by beetles.

"This is an unprecedented event. It's unprecedented growth in a single year," said Rick Cables, the head forester in the Rocky Mountain region.

If the beetles continue spreading at their current rate, they will kill the majority of the large-diameter lodgepole pines in Colorado within five years, foresters said.

Beetles have infested 1.5 million acres since 1996, when the outbreak began.

Closer to Southwest Colorado, foresters also found rapid growth in dead aspen stands. However, aerial surveys are less accurate for aspen, said Ingrid Aguayo of the Colorado State Forest Service.

Statewide, 334,000 acres of aspen have died, up from 138,000 acres in last year's survey. It's possible, though, that some of the trees in this year's survey actually died more than a year ago, Aguayo said. It will take a ground survey to get an accurate measure of aspen die-offs.

The dead aspen stands include a stretch north of U.S. Highway 160 between Durango and Cortez and areas along the West Fork of the Dolores above Stoner.

There are about 130,687 acres of aspen in the San Juan National Forest's Mancos-Dolores Ranger District, and in 2007 about 12 percent were considered dead or diseased. A few stands inventoried, mainly the lower elevation ones, had a mortality rate as high as 60 percent.

Surveyors also found dead blue spruce along the Continental Divide at Wolf Creek Pass. The beetles attacking spruce are different from the ones infesting lodgepole pines in Northern Colorado.



Forest problems in the south part of the state are nowhere near as bad as in the north, Cables said, with the exception of aspen.

"When you think of Colorado, you think of aspen," he said.

Foresters want to create a diversity of ages of trees. Right now, many of the state's aspen are the same old age, and they're having health problems, just like you would expect in a city if all the residents were 70 or 80 years old, Cables said.

He recommended prescribed burns and logging to clear out old aspen and make room for young ones.

It's a similar problem with lodgepole pines in Northern Colorado, but the devastation of the forests is much worse up north. The epicenter of the outbreak is Granby. Wide swaths of the forest have been killed south to Dillon and north past Steamboat Springs into Wyoming.

Last year, the beetles spread as far south as Leadville and hit the Front Range, where Boulder and Larimer counties saw 1,500 percent increases in infested trees, according to the survey.

Foresters do their yearly survey by airplane, plotting stands of dead trees on a global positioning system. It's an imperfect practice, performed at 115 mph, said Susan Gray of the U.S. Forest Service. But it's a useful tool to spot trends, even if the numbers are not accurate, she said.
